The Story

Where ink meets
melody

Dr. Dipanjali Deka inhabits a rare intersection — the scholar who sings, the singer who theorises. Trained in the rigours of the academy and the discipline of the raga, she moves between manuscript and performance, between the archive and the living breath of oral tradition.

Her doctoral research at Jawaharlal Nehru University — in the Department of Theatre and Performance Studies, School of Arts and Aesthetics — wove together the pluralities of nirguna bhakti through the oral song and poetry traditions of Lal Ded, Kabir, and Azan Fakir. Three voices, three traditions, one question: how does devotion survive in the human voice across centuries?

At the Archive and Research Centre of Ethnomusicology at the American Institute of Indian Studies, she deepened her engagement with the living archive — the songs that resist being pinned to paper, the prayers that breathe only when sung.

Today, as a Visiting Assistant Professor at the Centre for Writing Pedagogy, Krea University, she brings this richness into the classroom — teaching students not just to write, but to understand writing as a practice intimately linked to voice, community, and cultural memory.

"She interweaves the pluralities of nirguna bhakti through the oral song and poetry traditions of Lal Ded, Kabir, and Azan Fakir — three voices, one devotion."

The Voice

Music as a
way of knowing

Dr. Deka is not merely a scholar of music — she is a practitioner. A trained Hindustani Classical vocalist and a recognised voice on All India Radio, her engagement with music is simultaneously intellectual and embodied.

As a multilingual practitioner of Bhakti-Sufi music, she sings in the languages of the traditions she studies — Hindi, Kashmiri, Assamese — bringing an authenticity to her scholarship that only comes from living inside the tradition.
